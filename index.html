Ch 1

Introduction to CPE Laws

▼
Laws, Morality, and Ethics
  • Laws — Formal rules controlling behavior. Enforceable by the state and courts. Has narrower scope than morality/ethics.
  • Morality — Informal set of values, principles, beliefs, and ways of life. Rarely imposed by the state but socially pressured (e.g. Buddhism, Christianity, Stoicism).
  • Ethics — A discipline of philosophy; a reflective process based on beliefs, ideals, and purpose. Goes beyond law and morality; asks "What should I do?"
Ethics is NOT the same as morality or law — it examines moral systems to evaluate if they remain valid, and encourages open-ended inquiry about what is good and right.
Types of Law
  • Common Law — Based on court or tribunal decisions (case law / precedents)
  • Civil Law — Governs private matters (contracts, torts, property, family law)
  • Criminal Law — Governs arrest, detention, charging, prosecution of offenders. Example: Revised Penal Code
  • Procedural Law — Sets methods, rules, and procedures for court cases
  • National Law — Laws by which people are governed by the state
  • Imperative Law — Rules of action imposed by authority; main exponent: John Austin
Sources of Law
  • International Law — Generally recognized and accepted rules among nations
  • Custom — Social relations gave rise to several usages and traditions followed from ancient times
  • Religion and Morality — State converted moral and religious rules into laws
  • Judicial Decisions (Precedent) — Decisions by Supreme Court and High Courts recognized as laws
  • Delegated Legislation — Legislature of state delegates some law-making powers to lower bodies
  • Decree — Order from one having authority; a determination deciding what is to be done
  • Statute — Act of the legislature; the written will of the legislative body

Ch 2

Philippine IT Laws & Policies

▼
E-Commerce Law RA 8792
  • Also known as the Electronic Commerce Act of 2000
  • Approved by President Joseph E. Estrada on June 14, 2000
  • Recognizes role of ICT in nation-building; promotes ICT use in government and general public
  • Covers any electronic data message or document used in commercial or non-commercial activities — including domestic and international dealings, transactions, contracts
Key Provisions:
• Gave legal recognition to electronic data messages, documents, and electronic signatures (Sec 5 & 14)
• Allows formation of contracts in electronic form (Sec 16)
• Makes cable, broadcast, and wireless physical infrastructure within the telecommunications activity (Sec 28)
• Hacking/cracking is punishable by fine — max damage commensurate (Sec 33)
• Piracy through telecoms network that infringes IP is punishable (Sec 33)
• All existing laws also apply to e-commerce transactions (Sec 33)
• Parties given right to choose type and level of security methods (Sec 24)
Intellectual Property Code RA 8293
  • Protects and secures the exclusive rights of scientists, inventors, artists, and other gifted citizens to their intellectual property and creations
  • Highlighted through Senator Raul S. Roco (January 1, 1998)
  • Protects creations of the mind in commerce: Inventions, Literary & Artistic works, Designs, Symbols/Names/Images
Copyright — Two Types of Rights:
Economic Rights — Rights owner can prohibit or authorize: Reproduction, Public performance, Recording, Broadcasting, Translation into other language, Adaptation

Moral Rights — Right to claim authorship; Right to oppose changes that could harm the creator's reputation
  • Patents — Exclusive right granted for an invention (product or process) that provides a new way of doing something or a new technical solution
  • Trademark — A sign capable of distinguishing goods or services of one enterprise from another
Optical Media Act RA 9239
  • Also called Optical Media Act of 2003
  • Signed by Gloria Macapagal Arroyo on February 10, 2004
  • Reorganizes the video gram regulatory board; establishes country's fundamental principles for protecting and promoting intellectual property rights
  • Regulates production, mastering, replication, importation, and exportation of optical media
  • Optical Media — Storage medium where information (sounds, images, software code) is stored by mastering and/or replication using lens scanning mechanism (laser)
License: Every license prescribed by OMB; valid for 3 years; may be amended or renewed
Penalties: Closure of establishment + confiscation of manufacturing equipment + fine of not less than Php 300,000 but not more than Php 1,500,000
Data Privacy Act RA 10173
  • Popularly known as the Data Privacy Act of 2012 (DPA)
  • Guarantees people's privacy while ensuring free flow of information to foster innovation
  • National Privacy Commission (NPC) — country's privacy watchdog; administers and implements the DPA
Three Personas Involved:
• Data Subject
• Personal Information Controller
• Personal Information Processor

What needs to be protected: Name, Address, TIN, SSS No., Contact Numbers, Bank Account Details, ATM Card Details, Credit Card Details
Your Rights under DPA:
• Right to be informed — data should not be collected/processed/stored without explicit consent
• Right to access — ask if an organization holds your data
• Right to object — if processing is based on consent or legitimate interest
• Right to erasure or blocking — suspend, withdraw, order destruction
• Right to damages — claim compensation for inaccurate, false, unlawfully obtained data
• Right to file a complaint — if misused, maliciously disclosed, or improperly disposed
• Right to rectify — correct any inaccuracy or error in your data
• Right to data portability
DICT Act RA 10844
  • Established by Department of Information and Communications Technology Act of 2015
  • Ratified on May 23, 2016
  • Principal policy-making, planning, coordinating, implementing, and administrative body for ICT
  • IRR signed on October 17, 2016 by DBM Secretary Diokno and DICT Secretary Salalima
DICT Focus Areas: Policy and Planning · Improved Public Access · Resource-Sharing and Capacity-Building · Consumer Protection and Industry Development

ICT Agenda Priorities:
• National Broadband Plan — fiber optic and wireless deployment
• Free Wi-Fi in parks, plazas, public libraries, schools, government hospitals, train stations, airports, seaports
• National ICT Portal
Agencies transferred to DICT: ICTO, NCC, NCI, TELOF, NTTI, and all DOTC units dealing with communications
Cybercrime Prevention Act RA 10175
  • Enacted on September 12, 2012
  • Addresses crimes committed against and through computer systems
  • Contains penal substantive rules, procedural rules, and rules on international cooperation
Key Provisions of Cybercrime Act:
1. Illegal Access — Access to computer/application without permission
2. Illegal Interception — Interception of non-public computer data communications
3. Data Interference — Unauthorized tampering, destroying, deleting, deteriorating computer data or electronic documents
4. System Interference — Unauthorized alteration or deletion of computer data/programs to cause damage
5. Misuse of Devices — Cybersquatting
6. Computer-related Forgery
7. Computer-related Fraud
8. Computer-related Identity Theft — Unauthorized acquisition, abuse, transfer of identifying information
9. Cybersex — Willful engagement for favour/consideration via computer system
10. Child Pornography (RA 9775 — Anti-Child Pornography Act of 2009)
11. Libel
12. Aiding or Abetting
13. Attempt
14. Corporate Liability
Agencies attached to DICT (for cybercrime):
1. National Telecommunications Commission (NTC)
2. National Privacy Commission (NPC)
3. Cybercrime Investigation and Coordinating Centre (CICC)
Ch 3

Philosophical Framework & Cultural Issues

▼
Ethics vs Morality vs Law
  • Ethics encompasses more than just morality and law — it examines moral systems to evaluate if they remain valid
  • In an ideal world, ethical ideas influence the types of laws and moral systems a society adopts
  • Morality is rarely imposed by state; examples: Buddhism, Stoicism, Christianity
  • Laws are formal rules with narrower scope — enforced by state and courts
First written law: Code of Hammurabi, 1754 BC (Babylonian Laws) — an eye for an eye. Also related: Roman Law (Lex Romana).
Ch 4

Engineering Solutions & Societal Effect

▼
Social Responsibility of Engineers
  • The central focus of engineering is the application of scientific knowledge to meet societal needs
  • Engineering connects pure science to society — thus engineering has a social responsibility
  • Social responsibility = commitment to place public safety and interest ahead of all other considerations
Social Responsibilities (Examples):
• Ensure safety and well-being of the public
• Ensure society's funds and resources are well used
• Refuse to work on a particular project/company
• Speak out publicly against a proposed project
• Blow the whistle on illegality or wrong-doing
• Individual/organizational concern about engineering impact on society
Societal Problems Engineers Address
  • Overpopulation, Pollution, Global Warming, War, Stupidity, Disease, Habitat Destruction, Species Extinction
Sustainable Development (3 Pillars)
  • Economic Growth
  • Environmental Protection
  • Social Progress
Brundtland Commission: Sustainability = capacity to meet today's demands without jeopardizing future generations' ability to meet their own.
There are 17 UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs): No Poverty, Zero Hunger, Good Health & Well-Being, Quality Education, Gender Equality, Clean Water, Affordable Clean Energy, Decent Work & Economic Growth, Industry/Innovation/Infrastructure, Reduced Inequality, Sustainable Cities, Responsible Consumption, Climate Action, Life Below Water, Life on Land, Peace/Justice/Strong Institutions, Partnerships for the Goals.
Professional Engineer & Common Traits of Good Engineers
  • Problem solvers with firm grasp of fundamental principles
  • Analytical, detailed oriented, creative
  • Lifelong learners with core knowledge applicable to many areas
  • Strong written and oral communication skills
  • Time-management skills; good people skills; required to write reports
  • Participate in local and national discipline-specific organizations; attend seminars, workshops
  • Work in team environments; divide tasks into smaller manageable problems
Ch 5

Professional & Ethical Responsibilities

▼
Ethical Responsibility
  • Ability to perceive, comprehend, and act upon different principles and values based on norms within a certain area/situation
  • 7 Areas: Ethics, Law, Theories, Privacy, Accuracy, Property, Access
ACM Code of Ethics
1. General Moral Imperatives:
Contribute to society and human well-being · Avoid harm · Be honest and trustworthy · Be fair, no discrimination · Honor property rights including copyrights and patent · Give proper credit · Respect privacy · Honor confidentiality

2. More Specific Professional Responsibilities:
Strive for highest quality · Acquire and maintain professional competence · Know and respect existing laws · Give comprehensive/cautious job in planning · Design for actual consumers · Evaluate and test software · Be upfront about capabilities and limits · Pay attention to defaults · Develop communication skills
IIPTSA Code of Conduct
  • a. Integrity — Behave at all times
  • b. Confidentiality — Do not disclose confidential info of employers/clients without prior permission
  • c. Impartiality — No private profit if client/employer follows advice given
  • d. Responsibility — Full responsibility for work done; complete in agreed time and budget; alert client to late delivery
  • e. Relationship with IIPTSA — Do not seek personal advantage to the detriment of society
  • f. Non-discrimination — No illegal discriminatory practices; hire based on skills, experience, performance
  • g. Procedure — Anyone may lay a complaint against a member; level of member determines responsibility
Ten Commandments of Computer Ethics
  1. Thou shalt not use a computer to harm other people.
  2. Thou shalt not interfere with other people's computer work.
  3. Thou shalt not snoop around in other people's computer files.
  4. Thou shalt not use a computer to steal.
  5. Thou shalt not use a computer to bear false witness.
  6. Thou shalt not copy or use proprietary software without permission.
  7. Thou shalt not use other people's computer resources without authorization.
  8. Thou shalt not appropriate other people's intellectual output.
  9. Thou shalt think about the social consequences of the program you are writing.
  10. Thou shalt always use a computer in ways that ensure consideration and respect for other humans.
Ethics of the Filipino IT Professional
  • Promote public knowledge, understanding, and appreciation of IT
  • Consider general welfare and public good in performance of work
  • Advertise goods or services in a clear and truthful manner
  • Comply and abide by IP laws, patent laws, and related laws in respect of IT
  • Accept full responsibility for work undertaken; utilize skills with competence and professionalism
  • Make truthful statements on areas of competence
  • Not disclose or use confidential information without consent of parties concerned, except when required by law
  • Strive to attain the highest quality in both products and services
  • Knowingly participate in development of IT
  • Uphold and improve IT professionals' standards through continuing profession to enhance the IT profession
SE Code — 8 Principles (ACM/IEEE)
  • Principle 1 – PUBLIC — Act consistently with the public interest
  • Principle 2 – CLIENT AND EMPLOYER — Act in a manner consistent with the interests of client and employer
  • Principle 3 – PRODUCT — Ensure the highest professional standards possible
  • Principle 4 – JUDGMENT — Maintain integrity and independence
  • Principle 5 – MANAGEMENT — Promote ethical approach to management
  • Principle 6 – PROFESSION — Advance integrity and reputation of profession
  • Principle 7 – COLLEAGUES — Be fair and supportive of colleagues
  • Principle 8 – SELF — Participate in lifelong learning regarding practice of profession and promote an ethical approach
